    \section{Our chosen organization}
    IKEA Netherlands (from now on mentioned as IKEA) is a concern generating over 1.6 billion in revenue in the year 2024.
    The company has stores in 62 countries all over the world.
    Almost every household in the Netherlands has one or more products bought at IKEA, so this company comes close to our own lives.
    Their vision is to create a better daily life for as many people as possible.
    This goes further than interior, because they want to have a positive impact on the world.
    They also have an idea about how their business should work
    \uitspraak{to offer a wide range of well-designed, functional home furnishing products at prices so low that as many people as possible will be able to afford them.}{ikea_vision_values_nd}{1}

    The company IKEA was founded in 1943 in Älmhult  (Sweden) by Feodor Ingvar Kamprad.
    He started when he was 17 years old with his first intention, selling products to farmers.
    In 1947, the company started with furniture, and in 1951, it switched completely to furniture.
    In 1973, the first shop outside of Sweden was opened in Zürich, and in 1978, the first store opened its doors in the Netherlands.
    The classic Swedish meatballs were introduced in the year 1985.

    Even with a revenue of 1.6 billion, IKEA is available on the stock market; they have always been a family business, and in 1982, they became a part of the INGKA foundation.
    The work culture at IKEA is characterised by equality.
    They try to reflect the society, and their goal is to be as close as possible by 2027 \parencite{ikea_kansengelijkheid_2024}.

        When looking at the dimensions of strategic focus  (figure \ref{fig:strategic_focus}), IKEA would be placed in the top left corner of the diagram.
        IKEA has 3 main profit departments, being physical stores, online stores, and IKEA Food (IKEA physical stores and the online stores have the same inventory).
        This inventory ranges from ovens and stoves to beds but also includes furniture like tables and couches and even smart home solutions.
        Together with the fact that IKEA also provides a restaurant that serves not only coffee but also three-course meals too, one can conclude that IKEA’s competitive scope is \textit{broad}.
        This observation would place IKEA in the top half of the diagram.
    \end{multicols}

    Now, continuing to the x-axis of the diagram.
    One of IKEA’s key values is \textit{cost-consciousness} \parencite{ikea_culture_nd}.
    This means that IKEA wants to be as accessible as possible for everybody, even if one has little to spend.
    This key value is also acted upon.
    In the annual report of 2024, the board of directors stated the following:
    \uitspraak{The gross profit on sales landed on EUR 577 million, -1.9\% compared to the prior year. This is partly caused by lower sales prices to encourage customer spending, despite the increase in cost prices.}{ikea_nl_annual_2024}{}
    This statement shows that IKEA Nederland not only recognises the key values of IKEA, but they also act upon them, even if it means losing profit compared to the previous year.
    This would place IKEA into the \textit{low-cost} box and therefore in the top left corner of the diagram.

    Not only does IKEA focus on low costs for the customer, but it also differentiates itself on multiple levels.
    Think of the iconic Swedish meatballs, which are served at every IKEA restaurant throughout the country, or the distinctive blue and yellow colour combination.
    Recognition of this strong branding became evident during the World Branding Awards, where IKEA won the Best Brand award globally in the category Furniture – Home Living \parencite{world_branding_awards_nd}.
    However, IKEA does not only differentiate itself by its strong brand.
    The main characteristic of IKEA products is the fact that one has to build their just-bought product themselves.
    Few companies have managed to implement the DIY (Do It Yourself) into their products on the same scale as IKEA has.
    However, not only does the flat packaging (or DIY packages) differentiate IKEA from other companies, but it also cuts costs in both transport and assembly.
    Therefore, returning to one of IKEA’s key values, namely being cost-conscious.
    Although the diagram lets one assume a company can either focus on uniqueness or on low costs, IKEA shows that one can be unique while focusing on lowering costs.

    To conclude, IKEA belongs in the top-left corner of the strategic diagram (figure \ref{fig:strategic_focus}).
    This is the result of the large scale on which IKEA operates, together with its key value being cost-conscious.
    The biggest thing that makes IKEA unique (being their iconic DIY packages) also contributes to the cost-effective mindset, which concludes the decision that IKEA should be placed in the \textit{low-cost leadership }box.

    \subsection{Analyse the forces impacting the organisation}
    Using Porter’s five forces model, we will now analyse the external factors that might influence how IKEA should be structured to maintain a stable market share over the years.

    \subsection*{Competitive Rivalry}
    The number of rivals who operate on the same scale as IKEA (throughout the whole Netherlands) is low.
    However, IKEA does compete with small, local furniture stores that also focus on low prices.
    Think of second-hand furniture stores and discount stores.
    The advantage that IKEA has over its rivals is that every product of IKEA is new, and IKEA products among themselves are unified, meaning they match with each other.
    When buying the furniture from, for example, a discount store, the different products might not match as well.

    The furniture market of the Netherlands is mature, meaning that there will not be much growth or decline.
    This means that whenever all market shares are already divided among the companies, and if an organisation wants to expand, it has to take some market share from a competitor.
    This makes the competitive rivalry higher from the point of view of IKEA.

    This competitive rivalry increases due to the fact that the products of IKEA differentiate themselves mainly through the DIY concept, but apart from that, the products stay ordinary.
    This means that customers will only keep buying from IKEA when the prices stay low, meaning that the prices of IKEA must stay competitive in comparison to their rivals.

    To conclude, the competitive rivalry of IKEA is medium to high.
    This is mainly caused by the fact that the furniture market of the Netherlands has already matured, and the products of IKEA do not differentiate themselves enough for the customers to keep choosing them when the price increases.

    \subsection*{Power of Suppliers}
    IKEA has over 2100 different suppliers throughout the world \parencite{ikea_suppliers_nd}.
    Since IKEA Netherlands is part of IKEA Global, it has access to almost all 2,100 suppliers.
    Each of these suppliers does have to live up to the IWAY regulations of IKEA.
    Important aspects of the IWAY standard are implementing environmental improvements during production, fair compensation for employees and regulating the use of wood for IKEA products to make sure they live up to the environmental obligations of IKEA \parencite{iway_standard_nd}.
    Since not all suppliers may abide by these standards, IKEA’s pool of possible suppliers decreases.
    This results in more power for the suppliers that do abide by the IWAY standards since IKEA might not be able to find a good replacement.
    However, the scale of IKEA dissolves any real power of the suppliers.
    Because of IKEA’s large scale, the suppliers are dependent on IKEA.
    It is more than realistic to assume that IKEA is one of the main consumers of those suppliers.
    This means that they cannot simply leave IKEA, which will result in a low power of suppliers.

    \subsection*{Power of Customers}
    IKEA has 12 stores throughout the Netherlands \parencite{ikea_stores_nd}.
    These 12 stores together generated a turnover of 1.24 billion euros in 2021 \parencite{ikea_sales_figures_nd}.
    In 2021, all these stores, except Delft, welcomed 21.3 million paying customers \parencite{ikea_customers_nd}.
    The numbers of 2021 are analysed because this was the latest year in which IKEA published the number of customers they welcomed.
    Using these numbers, one can conclude that the average ticket price was around €58.21.
    This means that each customer contributes far below 0.0001\% to the total turnover of IKEA.
    Based on these numbers, one can conclude that the customer power of single customers of IKEA is exceptionally low.

    However, most customers do have one common divider, being the fact that they are extremely price sensitive.
    IKEA is known for their low prices, and that is one of the reasons it is attractive to customers, but as soon as another company has the same products for a lower price, a lot of customers might switch.
    Since most customers are greatly informed through online reviews and price comparisons, this is a real threat to IKEA.

    To conclude, one customer doesn’t have any power when looking at the financial numbers of IKEA.
    However, because of the large common divider between most of the customers being the low price, this group of customers could change supplier altogether, resulting in a problem for IKEA.
    Therefore, the power of customers is medium.

    \subsection*{Threat of Entrants}
    Entering the market as a new brand with the goal of competing with IKEA is immensely difficult.
    First of all, the scale of IKEA is already enormous, with 12 physical stores that run 7 days a week spread across the Netherlands \parencite{ikea_stores_nd}.
    Due to this scale, IKEA benefits from a lower purchasing price because of the ability to bulk purchase its products from its suppliers and therefore lowers its fixed costs.
    All those stores also have to be supplied every day to make sure their stock does not run out, which requires a complex distribution plan including routes, tactical placement of warehouses and the availability of enough trucks.
    Those warehouses not only have to be built or hired, but they also have to be filled with enough inventory to back up all the different stores.
    To get all this right as a startup would not only be complicated but also require an enormous initial investment that many startups will not be able to achieve.
    Together with the physical stores, the entrant would also need to set up an online webstore with the associated delivery options and an intuitive interface.
    This webstore is significant since IKEA made 486 million euros from only online sales in 2024 \parencite{ikea_nl_annual_2024}.

    In short, the threat of entrants into the market of IKEA is low.
    This is mainly due to the large initial investments and complex organisation of a company of this size.

    \subsection*{Threat of Substitute Products}
    When looking for a viable substitute for IKEA products, one should look at two factors.
    The first factor to consider is the relative price performance.
    Does the price of the product influence the buyer’s decision of the buyer much?
    In the case of IKEA, this is definitely the case, since their main selling point is the low price \parencite{ikea_culture_nd}.
    This means that if another product is cheaper, the customer is likely to switch.
    The willingness to go elsewhere is supported by the second factor, the sense that products are similar.
    IKEA products do not have a definite differentiation.
    Most of their products are very similar to other versions of the product.
    The only small differentiation of the product is the clear DIY booklets that are provided with every construction set.
    As soon as the DIY booklet of another company is as clear as that of IKEA, customers quickly perceive the products to be the same, resulting in a switch.

    In conclusion, the threat of substitute products is high for IKEA.
    This threat is mainly constructed by the fact that IKEA products do not differentiate themselves except for the low price they are offered at.

    \section{Information and communication technology}

    \subsection{Explain the role of ICT regarding the strategy and business model}
    Explain the role of ICT regarding the strategy and business model.

    \subsection*{ERP}
    Starting with the ERP programmes.
    These programmes are used to automate basic workflows, such as managing finances, supply chains and inventories \parencite{oracle_erp_nd}.
    To keep it manageable, only ERP programmes focused on inventory management will be considered.
    These ERPs track the inventory of each store, automatically order new supplies to prevent some items from being out of stock and analyse whether the sales are going as planned \parencite{erp_inventory_nd}.
    For large organisations such as IKEA, such an ERP is crucial to get a good overview of all 12 stores, since all these stores rely on the same inventory.
    When a human has to keep track of a large variety of products, items can be easily overlooked and therefore later be out of stock, which will result in unsatisfied customers.
    An ERP, however, can easily manage a large inventory, which enables IKEA to sustain one of its value propositions: a wide product range.
    An ERP also improves the relationship between IKEA and key partners such as the suppliers and logistics providers because these parties are getting the order on time, and no rush is needed.
    By decreasing the number of humans that are involved in the inventory process, one of the largest recurring costs can be lowered: the wages of employees.
    However, the disadvantage of an ERP system is the high initial cost.
    The organisation also has to change to get used to using the ERP, which might cause resistance within the organisation and take time to set up.

    \subsection*{Predictive analytics}
    In order to support the ERP, the second point will be discussed, namely, predictive analytics.
    While ERPs manage workflows based on available data, predictive analytics can take all those data points and make predictions for the future.
    When using such programmes, IKEA can gain an advantage compared to rivals (point one of Porter’s models), since IKEA can predict what customers are prone to buy and therefore make sure to change its inventory in time.
    Not only do predictive analytics give IKEA an advantage, but they also lower the threat of new entrants.
    Predictive analytics only work when there are enough data points, from a wide range of categories, to analyse.
    Since IKEA has so many stores across the Netherlands, a lot of data is saved, and therefore, the predictions will be more accurate.
    A new entrant does not have a large data set to base their predictions on, which will result in little to no useful predictions, which then gives IKEA an advantage to be quicker with new developments.
    The problem is that IKEA also has to keep the data up to date.
    This means that IKEA has to somehow track customers to analyse their buying patterns.
    To do this, a membership has to be implemented, and it has to be implemented in such a way that customers are willing to become members.
    For IKEA, this meant giving out free coffee and unlimited return time on all products \parencite{ikea_family_signup_nd}.
    These free options also come with a cost, which IKEA must be willing to accept to keep having useful predictions.

    \subsection*{E-commerce}
    The last technology that IKEA is using is e-commerce.
    E-commerce is a vital part of IKEA, generating over 486 million euros in 2024 alone \parencite{ikea_jaarrekening_2024}.
    When looking at the customer segments of the BMC, one can derive that a great part of IKEA’s target group is relatively young.
    This generation was raised with a mobile phone in their hand, and they also prefer to make purchases online.
    Online purchases are also valued higher since the customer has the feeling there is a better availability of stock and price \parencite{szymanski_2018}.
    The power of the customer does increase because customers can now compare prices more easily.
    First, they had to go to a physical store, remember the prices and go to another physical store to compare those prices.
    When all the prices and stock are available online, this barrier to comparing between stores has vanished.
    However, since a large customer segment prefers buying online, IKEA has to keep up by creating its own e-commerce to make sure it does not fall behind its rivals.


    \section{Sustainability topics}

    \subsection{Materiallity assessment}
    The internal stakeholders of IKEA are its employees, from both the stores and logistics.
    The second group of internal stakeholders is the INGKA group (the only shareholder of IKEA) and the top management of IKEA Netherlands B.V.
    The external stakeholders are the customers, suppliers, external logistics, the Dutch government, and competitors.

    The ESG list first environmental: the CO2 output, the use of eco-friendly materials, recycling and upcycling of old used products \parencite{ikea_sustainable_materials_2026}, waste management, energy usage inside shops and the reduction of packing material.

    The second social: the work environment in production, diversification and inclusion \parencite{ikea_equality_2026}, employee safety, product pricing for customers, and product safety \parencite{ikea_product_safety_2026}.

    The last governance: anti-corruption, ethical restocking, data and privacy security and whistleblower policy.

    Those ESG points can be ranked in terms of how important they are for IKEA; in this case, they will be measured in two point the importance for the organisation (financial and strategic) and the second point importance for the stakeholders.

    The most important few for both points are CO2 output, the use of eco-friendly materials, work environment in the production, product safety, ethnical restocking.
    The next few are a bit less important: recycling and upcycling, waste management, packaging reduction, employee safety, diversity and inclusion, product price for customers, anti-corruption, data and privacy security.
    The least important on this list are the energy usage in stores and the whistle-blower policy.
